Script

Good morning everyone. My name is Daniele, and together with Fabio and Frenci, we're going to take you on a journey through one of the most critical challenges in AI security today.

Imagine you've trained a powerful AI model — a model that catches malware, recognises faces, drives cars. It performs flawlessly in your tests. You deploy it. And then someone breaks it. Not by hacking your server. Not by stealing your data. By changing a single image by 0.007% — a difference your eyes cannot see.

That's what adversarial attacks are. And today, we're going to explain how they work, why they matter, and how we build models that can withstand them.

This field sits at the crossroads of machine learning theory, cryptography, and real-world security. It is not a niche academic topic — it is the difference between an AI system you can trust, and one that can be fooled by a sticker on a stop sign.

Let's begin.

📌 Point to the visual showing Clean → +ε noise → Wrong prediction. Let the audience look at it for a few seconds before speaking.
Script

Thanks Daniele. So, the fundamental problem is this: modern AI models are brittle in a very specific and dangerous way.

Look at this diagram. On the left, we have a stop sign. A perfectly normal photograph. A human sees it instantly. An AI model classifies it correctly — stop sign, 98% confidence. Great.

Now we add noise. Not visible noise — mathematically crafted perturbations, each pixel shifted by a tiny value, epsilon. The image still looks identical to you and me. But the model? The model now says: Speed Limit 45. With 91% confidence. It is completely certain — and completely wrong.

This is not a hypothetical scenario. Research shows that in optimised attacks, the success rate reaches 97% — meaning almost every image can be weaponised. And the perturbation needed is sometimes less than a fraction of a percent of the pixel value range.

The model has not changed. The image appears unchanged. But the outcome is catastrophic. This is the core problem of adversarial examples.

Delivery tips

  • Emphasise "completely certain — and completely wrong" with a brief pause between the two.
  • The 97% stat should sound alarming — raise your voice slightly.

Slide 3 of 9 · Types of Adversarial Attacks
~2 min
Evasion · Poisoning · Extraction White-box vs. Black-box FGSM · PGD · C&W Attacks happen at different stages
📌 Reference each card as you describe it. You can briefly hover over a card if presenting live — pace yourself, one attack type per ~20 seconds.
Script

Right. So not all adversarial attacks are the same. They differ in where they strike and how much the attacker knows. Let me walk through the main categories.

First — Evasion Attacks. These happen at inference time. The model is already trained and deployed. The attacker carefully crafts an input to slip through undetected. Think of it as forging a signature after the lock is already built.

Second — Poisoning Attacks. These are more insidious. The attacker corrupts the training data itself. A backdoor trigger is planted during training — invisible in normal operation, but when activated, it causes the model to misbehave on demand. This is especially dangerous in systems that retrain on user data.

Third — Model Extraction. Here the attacker doesn't touch the model at all. They query it repeatedly and reconstruct a clone. That clone is then used to craft attacks offline, with full access to the gradient.

Finally, we distinguish by White-Box attacks — where the attacker has full knowledge of the model — and Black-Box attacks, where they don't. Importantly, black-box attacks are still highly effective, which makes this problem very hard to defend against in production.

Delivery tips

  • Use your fingers to count — "First… Second… Third…" — it keeps the audience tracking.
  • The poisoning backdoor analogy is memorable — slow down there.
  • End with emphasis on "black-box attacks are still highly effective" — this surprises people.

Autonomous vehicles. Researchers demonstrated that by placing small, printed stickers on a stop sign — stickers that look like graffiti to a human — the car's vision system classifies it as a speed limit sign. Attack success rate: 240% higher than baseline attacks. A car doesn't brake. The consequences are physical.

Malware detection. 76% of ML-based antivirus systems can be evaded by adversarially modified malware — malware that retains its full function but fools the classifier into thinking it's benign. This is happening right now.

The estimated financial exposure from adversarial attacks on AI security systems reached $38.5 billion in 2024 — up from $12 billion just two years prior. This is not a research problem. This is an industry problem.

Slide 5 of 9 · What is Adversarial Robustness?
~2 min
Formal definition: ∀δ ‖δ‖≤ε: f(x+δ)=f(x) Vulnerable: 23% accuracy under attack Robust: 87% accuracy under attack ~15–20% clean accuracy cost
📌 Use the comparison slider — drag it slowly from left to right as you explain the gap between vulnerable and robust. This is the visual anchor of the whole talk.
Script

Great question. Adversarial robustness has a formal mathematical definition, but let me give you the intuition first, then the formula.

A model is robust if small, bounded changes to its input cannot change its output. If I nudge a photo of a stop sign by a tiny amount — within a small ball of perturbation — a robust model still says: stop sign. Always. Guaranteed.

Formally, we write this as: ∀ δ with ‖δ‖ ≤ ε : f(x + δ) = f(x). For all perturbations delta within a bound epsilon, the function output remains the same. That bound epsilon is what we call the robustness radius.

Look at this slider. A vulnerable model — one with no robustness — achieves only 23% accuracy when under attack. The same architecture, trained with robustness in mind, achieves 87%. That is a 64 percentage point difference on the same task, against the same attacker.

There is a cost. Typically, a robust model gives up around 15 to 20% of its clean accuracy to gain this protection. That is the robustness-accuracy tradeoff — and understanding this tradeoff is central to building real-world systems.

Delivery tips

  • Read the formula slowly — spell out "for all delta, such that the norm of delta is at most epsilon…"
  • Drag the slider while saying "23%… to 87%" — the physical gesture reinforces the gap.
  • The tradeoff is the nuanced insight — emphasise it's a deliberate engineering decision.

Slide 6 of 9 · Defense Techniques
~2 min
Adversarial Training (gold standard) Input Preprocessing Defensive Distillation Certified Defenses (provable)
📌 Click on each card to expand it as you speak about it. This paces the section naturally — one click per technique.
Script

Absolutely. We have four main families of defenses — and they sit on a spectrum from practical and empirical, to mathematically proven.

Adversarial Training. The most effective empirical defense. Instead of training only on clean data, we train on adversarial examples as well. We essentially simulate attacks during training so the model learns to be resistant. The most famous version is Madry's PGD-based adversarial training. The cost: significantly longer training time — but the payoff is real robustness.

Input Preprocessing. A simpler approach — transform the input before the model sees it. Apply Gaussian smoothing, JPEG compression, or a denoising network to strip the adversarial perturbations. It's computationally cheap, but clever attacks can often adapt around it.

Defensive Distillation. Train a second model on the soft probability outputs of the first — this smooths the decision boundaries and reduces the gradient information available to an attacker. It was one of the first principled defenses, though it has since been bypassed by stronger attacks.

Finally, Certified Defenses — the most principled approach. Techniques like Randomized Smoothing provide a mathematical proof that for any input within a given radius, the model's prediction cannot change. Not an approximation. A proof. This is the frontier of robustness research.

Delivery tips

  • Keep the same "one technique per beat" rhythm — pause between each name.
  • "A proof. Not an approximation. A proof." — say it exactly like that. The repetition is effective.
  • Click to expand cards as you go — it shows mastery of the tool.

Slide 7 of 9 · Metrics & Evaluation
~2 min
Clean accuracy vs. robust accuracy FGSM → PGD → AutoAttack hierarchy RobustBench leaderboard Certified radius ℓ∞
📌 Reference the bar chart on the left. The bars drop from green down to red — visually guide the class through the hierarchy of attack severity.
Script

Exactly. Measuring robustness requires a different mindset than measuring standard accuracy. Clean accuracy tells you how a model performs on normal data. Robust accuracy tells you how it performs when someone is actively trying to break it.

The industry standard benchmark uses a hierarchy of attacks. We start with FGSM — the Fast Gradient Sign Method — a single-step attack. Quick to compute, relatively easy to defend. Our model holds at 71% accuracy here.

Then PGD — Projected Gradient Descent — an iterative, multi-step attack. Much stronger. Accuracy drops to 58%. Then AutoAttack — a suite of four independent attacks used together, widely accepted as the most reliable evaluation benchmark. Accuracy: 47%.

We also measure the certified robustness radius — the size of the perturbation ball for which we can mathematically guarantee correct classification. And the attack success rate against our defended model — which sits at 13%. That means 87 out of every 100 adversarial examples fail against a well-defended system.

These numbers are tracked on RobustBench — the community leaderboard for adversarial robustness. It's the standard reference for comparing defenses fairly.

Delivery tips

  • Point to each bar as you name the attack — this anchors the numbers visually.
  • The hierarchy FGSM → PGD → AutoAttack should feel like a crescendo — each one harder.
  • End with "13% attack success rate" as a positive — frame it as the goal being achievable.

Script

Thank you, Fabio. Let me close with what I think is the most important takeaway from everything you've heard today.

Adversarial robustness is not a niche concern. It is not something to solve later, after the model is deployed. It is a first-class engineering discipline that must be built into AI systems from the ground up — especially when those systems make decisions that affect safety, security, or human lives.

We've covered a lot: how attacks work, what they've done in the real world, what robustness means formally, how we defend against it, and how we measure success. There is a rich research ecosystem here — over 3,200 papers published on this topic, a growing number of open-source tools, and a community leaderboard tracking progress in real time.

The good news is that progress is real. Well-designed defenses genuinely work. Certified guarantees are becoming practical. Attack success rates against defended models have dropped significantly.

But the adversary keeps learning too. Security is not optional. Robustness is not a feature — it is the foundation.

We are Daniele, Fabio, and Frenci. Thank you for your attention. We're happy to take any questions.

Delivery tips

  • Slow down for the final two sentences — "Security is not optional. Robustness is not a feature."
  • All three speakers should be standing visibly by the end — signals the presentation is complete.
  • After "Thank you", pause and wait — don't rush to sit down. Own the silence.
